Recycling Guide

How to properly recycle materials

About Recycling

Recycling involves converting waste materials into new materials and objects. It helps reduce the consumption of fresh raw materials, energy usage, and pollution. Recycling just one ton of paper can save 17 trees and 7,000 gallons of water.

What to Recycle

  • Paper and cardboard (newspapers, magazines, boxes)
  • Metal cans (aluminum, steel, tin)
  • Glass bottles and jars (all colors)
  • Plastic containers (#1-7, check local guidelines)
  • Clean food and beverage cartons (milk, juice)

What Not to Recycle

  • Food-contaminated items (greasy pizza boxes)
  • Plastic bags and film (can tangle machinery)
  • Styrofoam (not accepted in most programs)
  • Broken glass (wrap and dispose in trash)
  • Hazardous materials (batteries, chemicals)

Composting Guide

Turning organic waste into nutrient-rich soil

About Composting

Composting is the natural process of recycling organic matter into a valuable fertilizer that can enrich soil and plants. It reduces methane emissions from landfills and creates a closed-loop system for nutrients. Home composting can divert about 30% of household waste from landfills.

What to Compost

  • Fruit and vegetable scraps
  • Coffee grounds and filters
  • Eggshells (crushed decompose faster)
  • Yard trimmings (grass, leaves, small branches)
  • Paper towels and napkins (unbleached)

What Not to Compost

  • Meat, fish, and bones (attract pests)
  • Dairy products (can create odor issues)
  • Oily or greasy foods (disrupts decomposition)
  • Pet waste (may contain pathogens)
  • Diseased plants (can spread disease)

Landfill Waste Guide

Last resort for non-recyclable waste

About Landfill Waste

Landfill waste consists of materials that cannot be recycled or composted. Modern landfills are engineered to minimize environmental impact, but reducing landfill waste conserves resources and space. The average person generates about 4.5 pounds of landfill waste daily.

What Goes to Landfill

  • Plastic bags and film (unless recycled separately)
  • Styrofoam containers (not typically recyclable)
  • Broken glass and ceramics
  • Disposable diapers (unless using compostable brands)
  • Mixed material items (like chip bags)

What to Avoid in Landfill

  • Anything that can be recycled
  • Organic material that can be composted
  • Hazardous materials (batteries, chemicals)
  • Electronics (many contain recoverable materials)
  • Batteries (can cause fires)

Hazardous Waste Guide

Special handling for dangerous materials

About Hazardous Waste

Hazardous waste contains properties that make it dangerous to human health or the environment. Proper disposal prevents contamination of water supplies and soil. Many communities hold regular collection events for hazardous waste.

Hazardous Waste Items

  • Batteries (all types - alkaline, lithium, button)
  • Electronics (e-waste - computers, TVs, phones)
  • Paint and chemicals (oil-based paint, pesticides)
  • Light bulbs (CFLs, fluorescents contain mercury)
  • Medications (prevent water contamination)

What Not to Do

  • Put in regular trash or recycling bins
  • Pour down drains or toilets
  • Dispose with yard waste
  • Put in storm drains
  • Place in regular recycling

Plastic Bottle

Empty and rinse plastic bottles before recycling. Remove caps unless they're the same type of plastic. Most plastic bottles are made from PET (Polyethylene Terephthalate) which is widely recyclable.

Disposal Method: Place in recycling bin

Preparation: Rinse and remove caps

Alternatives: Consider refilling and reusing when possible. Use a reusable water bottle to reduce plastic waste.
